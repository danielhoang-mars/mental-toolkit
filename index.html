🌱 Workplace Wellbeing: The 8 Pillars
🌿 Nature
The restorative power of the outdoors
20-min rule: Just 20 minutes in green space measurably lowers cortisol and lifts mood. It doesn't need to be a forest. A park or a tree-lined street is enough (Hunter et al., 2019).
Lunch walk: Stepping outside at midday resets your circadian rhythm and gives your energy a real lift in the afternoon, particularly after a morning in front of a screen.
Desk plants: A University of Exeter study found that adding plants to your workspace can boost productivity by 15% and improve wellbeing. It also improves concentration and air quality.
Soft fascination: Watching clouds, water, or trees gives your brain what psychologists call soft fascination. It is a gentle, effortless form of attention that lets your mind rest and recover without switching off completely.
🌙 Sleep
Your single greatest performance tool
Consistency: Going to bed and waking at the same time every day, including weekends, is the single biggest thing you can do for sleep quality. It anchors your circadian rhythm.
Cool your room: Your body needs to drop its core temperature to fall and stay asleep. Keeping your bedroom at 16 to 18°C helps that happen naturally. A room that is too warm is one of the most common causes of broken sleep.
Screen cutoff: Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin, the hormone that tells your brain it is time to sleep. Stopping screens 90 minutes before bed lets melatonin rise and your mind start to wind down.
Duration: Most adults need 7 to 9 hours. Research shows that sleeping fewer than 6 hours for several nights significantly impairs cognitive performance, comparable to going one or two nights without sleep at all (Van Dongen et al., 2003).
🤝 Community
Connection is a health metric
"How are you, really?" When you ask, actually wait for the answer. Most people open up when they sense someone is genuinely listening, not just being polite.
Reach out first: People consistently underestimate how much a message means to someone who is struggling. Those who reach out rarely regret it. Those who don't, often do.
Eat together: Oxford researcher Robin Dunbar found that people who regularly share meals with others are happier, have stronger friendships, and report higher life satisfaction than those who eat alone.
Know your support at Mars: 150+ trained Mental Health First Aiders (MHFAs) can listen and signpost you to help. Your Employee Assistance Programme (EAP) through Lifeworks / Telus Health offers free, confidential counselling, legal advice, and financial guidance around the clock.
Notice sudden brightness: If someone has been low for a long time and then suddenly seems calm or even cheerful, take it seriously. A sudden shift in mood after prolonged sadness can be a warning sign. Check in with them directly.
🏃 Movement
Sitting less, moving more
20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, pause your screen, stand for 20 seconds, and look at something 20 feet away. This reduces eye strain and resets your posture.
Standing desk: Aiming for 2 to 4 hours of standing per day reduces back pain, lowers blood sugar spikes after lunch, and is linked to lower long-term cardiovascular risk. Build up by 30 extra minutes per week.
Walk the call: Take audio-only calls on the move. A 30-minute walk produces a measurable improvement in mood and reduces anxiety, regardless of fitness level. Stay aware of your surroundings and choose a safe, familiar route.
Desk stretches: Neck rolls, shoulder shrugs, and wrist circles every 90 minutes significantly reduce the tension headaches that come from prolonged sitting.
🙏 Gratitude
Recognition rewires the brain
Give specific praise: "You did X and it made Y happen" lands far more than a generic "well done". Specific feedback is more motivating, more memorable, and more meaningful. This applies to yourself too.
Gratitude journal: A study by Emmons and McCullough found that writing down three specific things you are grateful for each day shifts your brain's default attention toward positive experiences within about three weeks.
The 5:1 ratio: John Gottman's research shows that high-performing relationships, whether personal or professional, maintain roughly five positive interactions for every one critical or negative one. Teams below this ratio tend to have higher turnover.
☕ Breaks
Not all breaks are equal
Phone in your bag: Research from the University of Texas found that the mere presence of your phone on your desk reduces your cognitive capacity, even if you never touch it.
Change location: Stand up, go outside, or move to a different room. Changing your physical environment helps your brain register that the task is genuinely paused.
Soft fascination: Look at something moving. Trees, people passing, water. This gives your mind the kind of gentle, effortless rest it needs to come back sharper.
Time it: Your brain operates in roughly 90-minute cycles of focused capacity. A 5 to 15 minute break at the end of each cycle keeps your performance consistent through the day.
🥗 Diet
Food is mood. Your gut talks to your brain.
Eat little and often: Keeping your blood sugar steady prevents the mid-afternoon energy crash that many people mistake for needing more caffeine. Eating regularly maintains concentration and mood more reliably than willpower alone.
Gut-brain axis: Around 95% of the body's serotonin is produced in the gut, not the brain. Foods rich in fibre and fermented foods like yoghurt and kefir support the gut microbiome, which directly influences your mood, sleep, and stress response.
Hydrate first: Even mild dehydration, just a 1 to 2% drop in fluid levels, measurably affects mood, increases fatigue, and makes tasks feel harder. Drinking a glass of water before your morning coffee is one of the simplest ways to start the day sharp.
Omega-3 for cognition: Oily fish like salmon, mackerel, and sardines, eaten twice a week, provide omega-3 fatty acids that support brain cell structure and have been shown to reduce symptoms of low mood and anxiety.
Beat the glucose spike: A 10-minute walk after eating significantly reduces the blood sugar spike from your meal. This prevents the energy dip that typically follows lunch and helps you stay focused into the afternoon.

How to take a proper break
Step-by-step: not all breaks are equal

Switching to a different screen is not a break. Your brain needs genuine rest, not just a change of stimulation. Follow these steps for a break that actually restores you.

1
Put the phone in your bagResearch from the University of Texas found that the mere presence of your phone on your desk reduces your available cognitive capacity, even if you never touch it. Out of sight genuinely helps.
2
Change your physical locationStand up, go outside, or move to a different room. Changing where you are helps your brain register that the current task is genuinely on pause, not just minimised.
3
Choose soft fascinationLook at something moving. Trees, people passing, water. Psychologists call this soft fascination, a form of gentle, effortless attention that lets your mind rest without switching off completely.
4
Take 3 slow breaths before returningBox breathing (4 seconds in, 4 seconds hold, 4 seconds out) signals the nervous system to return to a focused, calm state rather than a stressed one. It takes less than a minute.
5
Time it: 5 to 15 minutes every 90 minutesYour brain operates in roughly 90-minute cycles of sustained focus. A short break at the end of each cycle keeps your performance consistent throughout the day rather than declining by the afternoon.
What doesn't count as a break: switching to social media, replying to messages, checking email, or watching short videos. These keep your nervous system in active mode. They are stimulation, not rest.
